Ptolemy's Coordinates

The second half of Ptolemy's Geography comprises a gazetteer: a listing of place names coupled with coordinates (latitude-longitude pairs). Ptolemy published this information together with the cartographic instructions at the beginning of the work so that his readers could create a map of the world known to the Greeks and Romans (the oikumene). Modern scholars have long used his gazetteer to help locate ancient sites and to try to figure out the ancient names of sites revealed through archaeology.

But other questions can also be asked. Where did Ptolemy get these coordinates and what does that say about the creation and dissemination of geographic information in antiquity? People often assume that Ptolemy compiled his gazetteer from earlier works now lost to us that, in turn, had been built from data that was gathered by surveyors or that was inferred from itineraries. But recent computational work by Leif Isaksen, an archaeologist and computer scientist at the University of Southampton in the United Kingdom, demonstrates that Ptolemy and his predecessors may have used a different approach.

Isaksen is preparing a complete treatment of the subject, which will soon appear in ISAW's digital journal ISAW Papers, but he has provided the following overview for the digital component of the Measuring Space exhibition.

Foo

Ptolemy's map is a composite, in which the size of the pieces are predetermined as a ratio in relation to the overall size of the oikoumene (which in turn is exactly one half of the earth's sphere (longitudinally). They are _not_ determined by aggregating survey measurements between places, even in well known provinces (Italy exactly one hour interval in length for instance. It seems likely that bigger regional blocs are in turn composed of smaller ones, but all of them seem to be related to the scheme in some way. This (theoretically) has the advantage of ensuring that they do not overlap one another as the map is constructed.

Bar

Given that these are not the meridians used in Ptolemy's final scheme, it suggests that he has inherited them from Marinos, who in turn is known to have extended the longitude of the oikoumene (i.e. ratio of its length to the planets size) from an earlier measurement. The division of the central asian provinces into four regions divided by the Indus, the Bagradas and the Euphrates, are highly suggestive of the 'sphragides' of Eratosthenes.

Flonkle

The use of a variety of different styles for listing coordinates suggest a variety of different sources. That in turn suggests that the coordnates were derived by marking them off of regional (i.e. chorographic) maps. None of the regions gives any indication of being derived from (written or travelled) itineraries, or indeed any other kind of source.
